It does appear that, despite Smith, the Court is still inclined to
void the application of generally applicable laws to religious con-
duct when the prohibited activity is engaged in, not by an indi-
vidual adherant, but by a religious institution. For instance, in
Hosanna-Tabor Evangelical Lutheran Church and School v. EEOC,347
the Court established a “ministerial exception” that precludes the
application of employment discrimination laws 348 to claims arising
out of an employment relationship between a religious institution
and its ministers.349 The Court found that even where such law is
a “valid and neutral law of general applicability,” and even if the
basis for the employment decision is not religious doctrine, the Free
Exercise Clause prohibits the application of an employment discrimi-
nation law, since enforcement of such law would involve “govern-
ment interference with an internal church decision that affects the
faith and mission of the church itself.” 350
Because of the broad ramiﬁcations of Smith, the political pro-
cesses were soon used in an attempt to provide additional legisla-
tive protection for religious exercise. In the Religious Freedom Res-
toration Act of 1993 (RFRA),351 Congress sought to supersede Smith
and substitute a statutory rule of decision for free exercise cases.
