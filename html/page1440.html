search or seizure without a warrant as an incident to a lawful ar-
rest has always been considered to be a strictly limited right. It
grows out of the inherent necessities of the situation at the time of
the arrest. But there must be something more in the way of neces-
sity than merely a lawful arrest.” 239
The Court, however, overruled Trupiano in United States v.
Rabinowitz,240 where officers had arrested the defendant in his one-
room office pursuant to an arrest warrant and proceeded to search
the room completely. The Court observed that the issue was not
whether the officers had the time and opportunity to obtain a search
warrant but whether the search incident to arrest was reasonable.
Though Rabinowitz approved of searches of the area within the ar-
restee’s “immediate control,” 241 it provided no standard by which
this area was to be determined, and extensive searches were subse-
quently permitted under the rule by the lower courts.242
